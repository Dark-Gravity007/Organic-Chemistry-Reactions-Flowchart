HBO Step 1 B₂H₆ adds across C=C — boron goes to less substituted C (anti-Markovnikov), H to more substituted C. Syn addition. → Step 2 H₂O₂/NaOH oxidises B→OH with retention of configuration. ∴ OH on less substituted carbon — pure 1° alcohol from terminal alkenes.
★ HBO vs Acid Hydration — Quick comparison:
• HBO (B₂H₆ / H₂O₂,OH⁻): OH on less substituted C, syn addition, no rearrangement, gives 1° alcohol from terminal alkenes.
• Acid Hydration (dil. H₂SO₄ / H₂O): OH on more substituted C (Markovnikov), prone to carbocation rearrangement, gives 2° or 3° alcohol.
★ Both reactions convert Alkene → Alcohol but with opposite regioselectivity — a very common CBSE comparison question.

Amines — Synthesis, Properties & Named Reactions
★ Basicity order: Aliphatic amines > NH₃ > Aromatic amines. In aqueous solution: 2° > 1° > 3° (steric vs. solvation effect). For aromatic amines: substituents on ring affect basicity via resonance.

▶ Gabriel vs Hoffmann — Quick Comparison

Gabriel Phthalimide:
• Input: Phthalimide + R–X
• Output: Pure 1° aliphatic amine (RNH₂)
• Carbon count: same as R–X
• Cannot make aromatic amines

• Input: Amide (RCONH₂)
• Output: 1° amine (RNH₂) with n–1 carbons
• Carbon count: one LESS than starting amide
• Works for both aliphatic and aromatic amides

Chemical Tests — CBSE Lab & Identification Reactions
★ These tests are used to identify functional groups in an unknown compound. They are very frequently asked in CBSE practicals and theory papers. Each test has a specific reagent, condition, and observable result.
Tests for Aldehydes vs Ketones
Tollens' Test (Silver Mirror)
★ Reagent: Ammoniacal silver nitrate [Ag(NH₃)₂]⁺ (Tollens' reagent). Mild oxidising agent — oxidises aldehydes but NOT ketones.
[Ag(NH₃)₂]⁺ / NaOH (warm)
Aldehyde → Carboxylate + Silver mirror ✓
Ketone → No reaction ✗
RCHO + 2[Ag(NH₃)₂]⁺ + H₂O → RCOO⁻ + 2Ag↓ + 4NH₃ + H⁺
★ Silver deposits on the inner wall of the test tube as a bright mirror — key observation.
Fehling's Test (Brick-Red PPT)
★ Reagent: Fehling's Solution A (CuSO₄) + B (NaOH + Na-K tartrate). Mixed fresh before use. Tests aliphatic aldehydes only — aromatic aldehydes and ketones do NOT react.
Fehling's A + B / Heat
Aliphatic Aldehyde → Cu₂O ↓ (Brick-red PPT) ✓
Aromatic Aldehyde → No PPT ✗
Ketone → No PPT ✗
★ HCHO (formaldehyde) also gives a silver mirror with Tollens but gives CO₂ instead of brick-red with Fehling's — exception to note.
Iodoform Test (Yellow PPT)
★ Reagent: I₂ + NaOH (or NaOI). Positive for compounds with CH₃CO– group (methyl ketones) and CH₃CH(OH)– group (secondary alcohols with methyl). Also positive for ethanol and acetaldehyde.
I₂ / NaOH (warm)
CHI₃ ↓ (Yellow PPT, antiseptic smell) ✓
Positive for: CH₃COR (methyl ketones), CH₃CHO (acetaldehyde), CH₃CH(OH)R (2° methyl alcohols), CH₃CH₂OH (ethanol).
Negative for: HCHO, other aldehydes, non-methyl ketones, 1° alcohols (except ethanol).
★ Iodoform test on an alcohol first oxidises it → if positive test emerges, it was a methyl carbinol.
Tests for Alcohols (1°, 2°, 3°)
Lucas Test — Distinguish 1°, 2°, 3° Alcohols
★ Reagent: Lucas Reagent = Anhydrous ZnCl₂ + Conc. HCl. Reaction produces an alkyl chloride (insoluble, forms cloudy layer). Rate depends on carbocation stability.
ZnCl₂ / Conc. HCl (room temp.)
3° alcohol → Turbidity immediately ✓
2° alcohol → Turbidity in ~5 min ✓
1° alcohol → No turbidity at room temp ✗ (reacts on heating)
★ Mechanism: SN1 via carbocation. 3° carbocations form fastest. Works only for alcohols with 5 or fewer carbons (larger ones are already insoluble).
Cerric Ammonium Nitrate Test (CAN)
★ Reagent: Ceric Ammonium Nitrate — (NH₄)₂Ce(NO₃)₆. General test for the presence of an OH group (alcohols and phenols).
(NH₄)₂Ce(NO₃)₆
Red / Orange colour ✓ (if OH present)
Na Metal Test — Presence of Active H
★ Alcohols and phenols react with Na metal to give H₂ gas (brisk effervescence). Distinguishes R–OH from R–X.
Na metal
H₂↑ (brisk effervescence) ✓
2R–OH + 2Na → 2RO⁻Na⁺ + H₂↑. Phenols react more vigorously than alcohols (more acidic).
Tests for Amines — Distinguish 1°, 2°, 3°
Hinsberg's Test (Benzenesulphonyl Chloride)
★ Reagent: Benzenesulphonyl chloride (C₆H₅SO₂Cl) + aq. KOH. The key difference is whether the sulphonamide formed has an N–H bond (acidic enough to dissolve in KOH) or not.
C₆H₅SO₂Cl + KOH (1° amine)
Sulphonamide with N–H → dissolves in KOH ✓
C₆H₅SO₂Cl + KOH (2° amine)
Sulphonamide, no N–H → insoluble PPT ✓
C₆H₅SO₂Cl + KOH (3° amine)
No reaction ✓ (no N–H available, steric hindrance)
Why 1° sulphonamide dissolves: N–H on the sulphonamide is acidic (withdrawing –SO₂– group lowers electron density) → KOH deprotonates it → water-soluble salt.
Why 2° sulphonamide does not: No N–H to be deprotonated → stays as insoluble oily precipitate.
Carbylamine Test — Test for 1° Amines Only
★ Reagent: CHCl₃ + alc. KOH + heat. The reaction produces an isocyanide (carbylamine) which has an extremely foul, nauseating odour. Positive only for 1° amines — both aliphatic and aromatic.
CHCl₃ + 3KOH (alc.) / Δ (1° amine only)
R–N≡C (Isocyanide) — foul smell ✓
Carbylamine Test
CHCl₃ + KOH (2° or 3° amine)
No isocyanide smell ✗
Full equation: R–NH₂ + CHCl₃ + 3KOH → R–N≡C + 3KCl + 3H₂O
★ The foul smell of the isocyanide is the key observation to write in your answer. Mention it explicitly.
★ 2° and 3° amines do NOT give this reaction — used to distinguish 1° from others.
